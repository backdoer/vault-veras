# Brian Call

Tracked metrics:
- Week by week analysis
- Break down by position and location
- Wants to see a total dollar amount of how much they spent on agency and/or saved on agency
- They'd track if people showed up later or no-showed to try to help save 

They will often fill lower end roles with higher end roles if needed - they often don't care how much they're spending as long as they get the talent they need.

Likes the idea of tracking people who came from agencies and eventually attempting to hire them away

Some people are going to use the program just to feel good about themselves even though they're not actually going to make meaningful change

Scheduling is 40% of the way there

Have a preferred agency partner

He thinks we could potentially integrate with Elite (staffing agency) and his people could be loaded in and take shifts on a higher price point.

He's used Elite in Michigan, Tennessee, Ohio, Virginia

He starts with how many open shifts they have and wants to see their deficiencies - they meet weekly and go over it

On one of his last engagements, they were using 80% agency and when he left, had had them at 0.

Very hands on process - spends the majority of his time with the 20% who are doing 80% of the work. Sees it as a leadership/management effort. 

Teaches them how to:
- Interview
- Select
- Create mentors - give them some skin in the game
- Create scorecards
- Make everything they track visible

Referrals:
- Automate the process
- Referral leaderboard

He uses perm to get them off of agency. One of the hardest parts of tackling perm scheduling is knowing the competitive landscape. He things OnShift can do the job well, but it's cumbersome. Everyone uses it because it's out there, but no one really likes it. Very few usable reports - not very visual. 

If they're using OnShift, he thinks our hook is going to be:
- scheduled overtime
- open positions
- does it better
- easy to transition

Lead with agency reduction which transitions right into agency reduction through scheduling (40-50% of the problem). Onboarding, education, communication, leadership are the other half of the problem. "People leave when they don't know how to be successful." If people aren't onboarded correctly, people will leave. The majority of the time, people leave within 60-90 days if they leave.

Tries to create action plans around how to improve things like:
- Supplies and equipment
- Teamwork
- Scheduling

"If we're understaffed, I don't trust you. If I don't see effort to change things, I don't trust you."

He constantly meets with employees to try to understand the problems and then makes action plans (visible and public) to fix them.

He was confused by which certs were relevant. He liked the idea of incorporating perm employees in as well. He'd also like to see if people had completed their orientation, training, onboarding etc.

He recommended having a "Do not hire" list. And incorporating it into perm.

He doesn't see any value in the star rating.

"Unless you have enough PRN staff, you'll always have to dip back into agency."

You need FT, PT, and PRN. The least important is the PRN pool. You need to implement some kind of check on the PRN to hold them accountable. Otherwise, they just choke your payroll with fees, etc. He said having your PRN employees in the same system as your FT/PT causes issues.

Most of the time, PRN pools are just a pain. You're just trying to track them down to get them to take a shift. He likes the idea of managing a PRN pool within a wider scope of also handling perm and scheduling.

He never hires someone who is still locked by an agency contract.

15% of people who come through from agency end up being open to being recruited. Especially if they feel valued, they like the facility, the people, etc.

You HAVE to have a few solid PRNs. The last places he was at, they only had 3-4 valuable PRNs to 70-80 FT/PT.

Stack ranked pain points:
1. Full time hiring
2. Full time retention
3. Full time scheduling

You're going to see big overages when you hire an RN to fill a LPN role or when you have people work overtime.

RNs prefer working at a hospital - there's a higher respect associated to it.

Make your current employees like you and trust you and then they will refer their friends.

Trickle down / Transitive solutions. Is there a way we can help them improve and build their culture? It would trickle down to helping solve the hiring, scheduling and retention issues.

Provide scorecards that he can put in the break room. Success metrics. Hired x people in the last x days. Fully off agency for the last x days. Fully onboarded. x days since the last accident. Shout people out and thank them. This person hasn't cancelled a shift in x days.

5-10 things he'd want measured and recognized and goals for:
- Go to $0 agency used (also, incremental gains)
- All PT/FT positions filled
- No call-offs (cancellations)
- x% of shifts fully staffed
- Shifts in a row without a call-off

Is there a way we can gameify it? Kind of like motivosity.

There aren't any scheduling tools that are knocking it out of the park. None of them are looking at it relevant to building a culture. 

There's a negative relationship between culture and temps/agency. He'll isolate all of the temps in one area of the facility so they don't intermingle. Keeps operations and employees separated between FT/PT and temps. When a temp comes through that's good, they'll try to recruit them.

How did management and executives respond to your pitch and value prop? Surrounding approaching hiring, scheduling and retention with a focus on culture. He was mainly handling inbounds - people came to him. Never really had to put together a pitch deck. A lot of the time, people think their culture is already great. He would soon discover that it is not great.

Facebook and Instagram recruiting are powerful. For the most part, facilities are only using 1-2 recruiting platforms.

He didn't know what PAC was. He said it costs $5-7K every time someone leaves. 

He had a VP of ops who loved Apploi so he let her us it. He would just use indeed for other locations. He's willing to make an intro to this VP of ops. He's also willing to line us up with some others he's worked with too.

Really likes drill down on reports.
