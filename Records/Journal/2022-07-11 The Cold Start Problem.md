# The Cold Start Problem

Network of atomic networks

Networks are like animal populations. Have to find the sweet spot of population. 

Network effect: simply put, the more people you get on the network, the more powerful it is 

Cold start: one side of the market is there but the other isn’t

Atomic networks: the smallest stable network you can have where everyone will stick around. For us, it’s the schools, their students and employer partnerships. They have their own dynamics and effects.

Start with the supply side. For us that’s employers. 

Measure results and metrics at the atomic network level - not the aggregate level. 

Atomic product: the smallest, simplest product that could possibly add value. Capture an atomic network with an atomic product.  Cutting alumni allows us to focus on one niche - we can always expand later once we have critical mass. 

“A distinctive feature of network products is that they often do one thing well.” Zoom’s whole value prop is that it’s way simpler and easier to use than Skype and the rest of the other solutions in the space. We need to continually trim the fat. 

Should we slow down and nail one network at a time? One school and its partners. Our GTM loop. I think Utah is too big for our atomic market. Per school!! Atomic markets are often smaller than you’d think. How do we prevent churn at our other 9 schools? 

Having 1000 concentrated users in the same atomic network is much more powerful than having 1000 users spread out across several different networks where they can’t interact. 

The more users you need to get to an atomic network, the harder it is to create.

Stages:
1. Cold start problem
2. Tipping point
3. Escape Velocity
4. Ceiling
5. Mote 


Slack started with tinyspec (a gaming app) which flopped. Slack was an internal tool they’d built. 

Winner takes all

Anti network effects: 
- chain reaction of people leaving as they’re not finding the value they want

If you don’t properly nurture and strategically scale your network, you’ll fall victim to your network caving in on itself (employers will stop posting). We need to account for this sooner than later. 

Target your niche and nail it! Once you nail one atomic network, you know you can do it again. 

Threshold at which people will stick around (for slack, that was about 2000 messages - at which 93% of customers stick around). There’s a point of diminishing return in investment here. Find the threshold. 

Teamchat failed as an attempted network play. Did they attempt to scale too early? Gotta focus on the atomic network! 

Credit cards were invented in the 50s by BOA in Fresno and Modesto. They started with those cities. Facebook started by targeting campuses. Slack started by creating atomic networks of 3. 

Schools are the catalysts and foundation of our atomic networks. 

Employers are the hard side! The most is expected from them. You have to nail the hard side. Consumers are often cheaper and easier to maintain. 1/10/100 rule. 1% of people create - 10% of people synthesize - 100% of people benefit. 

With tinder, attractive girls were the hard side. They were the ones every guy came for and hoped for. Guys swipe on 45% of girls whereas girls only swipe on 5% of guys. Tinder catered to the hard side by:
- allowing users (girls) to un-match 
- allowing girls to swipe 
- they can stop swiping 
- Privacy-focused & Facebook integration

Bumbl went even further with this strategy by only allowing girls to message first. 

“If a piece of content was created and if no one saw it, would the creator be disappointed? If the answer is ‘yes’ then social feedback is a key value.” Status is a massive motivator. It’s what motivates the small, prolific group of Wikipedia writers. And YouTube and TikTok creators.
